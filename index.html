The Challenge

Bicol faces interconnected crises that traditional aid cannot solve.

Climate Vulnerability

Recurring typhoons and floods destabilize agricultural income, leaving communities in perpetual recovery mode. Traditional livelihoods are no longer viable.

Undervalued Women's Labor

Women's fiber processing and weaving work generates minimal income. Skills are devalued, labor is invisible, economic power remains concentrated elsewhere.

Low Local Value Capture

Bicol produces 33.9% of the Philippines' abaca but captures only 1% of global value. Raw materials are exported; profit flows outward.

Our Approach

A localized value chain that keeps economic power in the hands of producers.

From Crisis to System

Building what disaster relief cannot provide: sustainable economic infrastructure.

Why Babayi Exists

Babayi emerged from the recognition that disaster relief—while necessary—cannot build long-term resilience. Communities need economic systems that can withstand shocks, not just recover from them.

We built a women-led weaving ecosystem that addresses root causes: climate vulnerability, undervalued labor, and extractive economic structures that drain local wealth.

Our model generates revenue from purpose-driven consumers and ethical partners, sustaining operations while reinvesting in social impact programs including disaster preparedness and psychosocial support for children.

This is not charity. It is a viable business model that proves profitability and community welfare can coexist.

How It Works

Transparent partnerships across the entire value chain.

From Fiber to Market

Abaca farmers receive training in fiber processing, moving from selling raw materials to producing value-added products. Women handloom weavers work on traditional looms using upcycled cotton threads and natural fibers.

Home-based artisans handle sewing and finishing, creating flexible work that continues even during climate disruptions. No factories. No middlemen. Direct partnerships with fair compensation.

Products are marketed to purpose-driven consumers who value heritage, sustainability, and measurable social impact. Revenue reinvests in the ecosystem—building resilience, not dependency.

Leadership

Founded by Bicolana professionals who understand both the problem and the market.

Babayi was founded by Yani and Mira, two daughters of Bicol who recognized that their communities needed economic infrastructure, not just emergency aid.

We built Babayi with a dual mandate: create market-competitive products that consumers actually want, while building a business model that centers community welfare and environmental sustainability.

Our approach combines deep cultural knowledge with business acumen. We know these communities. We know these markets. We know how to make both work together.
